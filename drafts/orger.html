It would be cool to be capable of always remembering and instantly recalling information you've interacted with, metadata and your thoughts on it. Until we get augmented though, search is as close as you can get.

When you actually try that on practice though you run into obstacles. I'll give some I've personally been overcoming as examples, but I'm sure you'll come up with your own:

  • convenience of access, e.g.:
    • to access annotations on my Kobo ebook I'd need to actually fetch my reader and tap on e-ink touch screen. Not much fun!
    • if you want to search over annotations in your PDF collections… well good luck, I'm just not aware of such tool (let alone the fact that many pdf viewers wouldn't even let you search throught highlights) TODO link annotations
    • there is no easy way to access all of your twitter favorites, people suggest using hacks like autoscroll extension.
  • searching data, e.g.:
    • often just not available at all or not incremental, e.g. on instapaper, you can't restrict search to highlights
    • builtin Ctrl-F browser search sucks for the most part: you don't get preview, it's not easy and quick to navigate
  • data ownership and liberation, e.g. what happens if the service is down or gone or banned by your government?
    • 99% of services don't have support for offline TODO see #offline? it may or may not be worrying to you depending on how paranoid you are TODO towards? apocalypse, but my take on it is that at least I'd have my data :)
    • if you delete a book on Kobo, the highlights just disappear TODO FIXME investigate maybe that only happens on sideloaded books? still worrying TODO also motivation for incremental backups
    • in 2018, instapaper was unavailable in Europe for several month (!) due to missing the GDPR deadline

TODO often you don't even quite remember where to search. E.g. you ran into TODO mypy issue for instance. You vaguely recall seeing it somewhere else a year ago. Was it github? Or question on stackoverflow? Or was it just a todo comment in one of your personal projects? In essence, ideally you'd want some sort of search engine, but over your personal data, so you'd have unified way of accessing all of it.

In essence, in many cases the service controls the way they present you information. That doesn't need to be that way, and I feel that separating that would let services on providing good service and community to develop tools for consuming it.

¶3 TODO Reddit reader

This one I particularly find so useful it deserves a separate TODO mention? . I have been sustainably using for almost a year now, so I believe it could be useful to many people.

I tend to be curious about lots of different topics so I'm subscribed to over 100 subreddits.

For the most part I'm reading Reddit passively and I can't afford spending much time on Reddit every day, so my system for years have been reading updates for a week once a week.

For few select subreddits that most interest me (e.g. quantifiedself) I read all wekkly updates, for the rest I just read weekly top.

I tend to do it from my phone on the go/in transport or while I'm waiting somewhere, so the concentration is not exactly perfect and many things are awkward to read or browse on phone.

So I tend to favorite interesting submissions (judging by the title) without reading them instead, and later once in a while when I'm at my desktop I have a deeper look at whatever I favorited.

This is however gets a little clumsy.

  • TODO items in 'saved' can't be rearranged and the only sort available is by 'saving time'.

This would be ok if every time you processed your saved items you cleared all of them, but that's just not very realistic. Some things take higher priority; some things just give you few thoughts now and you wanna action on it later. So every time you process saved submissions, you end up going through same things all over again, and you have to remember where you stopped last time. That's quite distracting!

  • sometimes posts/comments get deleted so if you look at saved item months later, you'd not know what did you save.

While there are ways to read removed reddit comments, it's a bit distracting to do too and with orger you'd always have an original version of the item.

To be more specific, here's my typical workflow with reddit.org file:

  • I'm running reddit data exporter and reddit.py every night automatically (via cron).
  • when I feel like reading some Reddit, I open reddit.org and jump to new items at the end of file (I can tell them from old by not having priority), and go through them
  • some things just get refiled immediately. E.g. if I saved something on /r/Art normally I'd just move it to dump.org TODO
  • what isn't refiled, I mark with priority
  • when everything is prioritized, I select the whole file, call org-sort-entries and sort by priorities.
  • now that everything looks a bit more manageable, I can action on #A and #B items properly.

Some I just read and archive. Some give me thoughts or ideas which I can add under the corresponding org-mode heading. Some things just require actually acting on them (e.g. new library release), so I can mark it as TODO and schedule so it shows up in my agenda.

  • also, once in a while I'll look through items with lower (#C / #D) priorities and bump them or archive if they become irrelevant.

Needless to say when I'm searching through all of my information (TODO link to search.org?), reddit saved items pop up as well.

The only minor downside of my particular implementation is that items are not removed from TODO on reddit. One could probably implement this with API call on archiving/marking item as done or something, I just didn't find it too big of a deal for me.

¶one-way data flow

Implementing feedback from emacs, e.g. editing github comment when you edit the corresponding orger item would be really cool, but it requires considerably more effort and would only work within emacs.

¶race condition (TODO what would be better name? conflict?)

Unfortunately there is a little space for TODO race condition if orger appends something while you're editing file. Orger tries to detect emacs and vim swap/lock files, but it's if you're very unlucky or using different setup it's still possible. Hopefully your text editor warns you when the file had been overwritted while you were editing it (e.g. as emacs does).

Also I run orger cron jobs at night so it's quite unlikely to overlap with editing anything.
