Promnesia is a browser extension (Chrome/Firefox/Firefox mobile) which serves as a web surfing copilot, enhancing your browsing history, improving the web exploration experience and integrating it with your knowledge base.

The repository contains some demos and the setup guide, this post is more of a motivation and the story of what has led me to work on it.

¬∂it's not just about web browsers

If you're using a native phone app (say, for Reddit or Twitter), you history either isn't logged anywhere or stored locally on your phone.
this is why I emphasise browsing history, not browser history
On Android, it's likely to be in an Sqlite database in /data/data/app.name. This directory is not accessible to normal users, unless your phone is rooted. Just think about how ridiculous it is. It's your own data, yet your OS babysits you and prevents your from accessing it.
rooting Android phones ranges from "somewhat painful" to "very painful"

As a specific example, Reddit Slide app keeps the view history in /data/data/me.ccrama.redditslide/SEEN. Even if you root your phone and can access the database, because TODO: model is flawed something something the app isn't persisting the data in the database, it's merely a cache TODO latest few weeks or something? So if you want your complete history, you have to backup this database regularly, keep the snapshots and somehow reconstruct it.

You may dismiss this as data nitpicking, but when all of your phone apps are doing this, you're missing out on quite a lot of information.

¬∂it's scattered and siloed

Continuing the previous point:

  • for the most part, you can't access history in your phone apps

  • lots of data which is ought to be counted as web history is scattered across silos

    It sits behind the APIs (best case), GDPR and manual exports (worst case) and hard to get hands on even if you're an experienced software engineer.
    #sadinfra

    It idles on your filesystem in markdown and org-mode files.

Even regular browser history is not easy to get hands on:

  • Firefox used to silently 'expire' your browser history

  • Chrome deletes history older than 90 days

    The only way to access older history I'm aware of is Google Activity and Takeout.

  • Google Takeout quietly recycles your history

  • Migration is limited to the most popular browsers

    The retention limits the migration as well. E.g. if you migrate from Chrome to Firefox, the history older than 90 days is locked and siloed in your Google Account.

  • You're gonna have hard time if you're not using Google/Mozilla sync

    Your history will be scattered across devices and lost on OS reinstalls.

    There are ways of selfhosting Firefox sync, and (allegedly) even Chrome sync, but, as you can imagine, it's quite tedious.

¬∂'Unlimited history' extensions

There is a family of Chrome extensions similar to History Trends Unlimited, which try to make up for Chrome 90 day retention by keeping the history in IndexDB. The only problem they solve is the said retention, and it ends up as a yet another silo.

But at least, thousands of users means there is a demand for such functionality.

¬∂Worldbrain Memex

Worldbrain Memex is a very cool browser extension. It's open source, keeps your history, supports tagging, highlights, annotation and even full-text search. By all means, try it!

The only problem is that Memex is that it's also kind of a silo. I really hate using the word "silo" here, because it's (TODO kindo of) an insult. If anything, Memex is probably the nicest silo I know of. It keeps data locally, it's got data imports from other services.

But nevertheless, if you commit to using it, you have to move the data into Memex, and confom it to its model.

If for some reason you can't or don't want to use Memex for annotating and recording all of your history (e.g. on some of your devices) ‚Äì you TODO. TODO see the point that "it's not about the browser"
