Promnesia

A quest for fixing browser history

Draft stage: mostly ready in terms of structure? Needs polishing and spell/grammar check? Not sure if it should be split into several pages? And there is also more stuff I'd like to write, but it's already long enough…

Promnesia is a browser extension (Chrome/Firefox/Firefox mobile) that serves as a web surfing copilot, enhancing your browsing history, improving the web exploration experience, and integrating it with your knowledge base.

The repository contains some demos and the setup guide, this post is more of the motivation and the story of what has led me to work on it.

¶1 I want my singularity!

I was raised on science fiction, and growing up, I've been dreaming about the technology drastically changing our lives. Artificial intelligence, augmenting the brain with a math co-processor, head-up display, neural interfaces, having perfect reaction and memory – many of you are on the same page and know the drill.

Years had passed, I became a software engineer and had realized how far are we from all these fancy technologies I wanted. So my TODO: dreams? ambitions? need a better word have become more modest, and I've chosen a more realistic and plausible target: using my digital trace like browser history, webpage annotations and my personal wiki to make up for the limited memory.

I'm exploring lots of information on the Internet, and it feels wasteful to use my meat resources TODO: tag? to keep track of it. Web browsers tracks it automatically, without any manual and conscious effort.

Surely we can benefit from the immense computing and memory capabilities of the computer?

So I figured that I wanted:

¶2 Ok, maybe a web assistant at least?

A web surfing companion. A copilot. A Memex. A Remembrance Agent?

Call it whatever you like, I wish it was a concept with a widely accepted name. Something that will:

  • aid me with information processing

    Keep track of what I've read, when, on what device, and for how long.

  • show me my annotations and highlights, within the original page

    I don't want to keep wondering whether I got my notes and thoughts stashed in some app, I want the computer to let me know instead.

  • unify my scattered and siloed bookmarks

    Firefox bookmarks; Reddit/Hackernews saves; "Watch later" on Youtube, saved links in your instant messenger.

    These are the same damn thing, yet there is no way to oversee and process them through a single interface. TODO: JB something about 'universal queue'?

  • connect with my online presence: chats and social networks

    If I've tweeted about a page, surely this fact should be present in the browser history? In fact, it's way more important than a simple browser visit.

  • help me to explore new information and prioritize it

    There is so much awesome stuff on the Internet! The biggest problem I have is picking the next cool link to dig into.

    I wish there was something that could TODO: with a single click? show me, who of my friends, or of the people I follow, have read a certain link. What did they think? Have they posted about it, annotated, or something else? Do they follow the author?

  • bridge the gap between the browser and my private wiki

¶3 Browser history is broken

Web browser history is a rich source of potentially useful information. Just think about it: it's zero effort lifelogging. It contains exact timestamps and links which basically address information.

To a large extent, URLs express relationships TODO: like person/group/post/comment/etc.. elaborate, and again, with pretty much zero effort – it's the way web works. Seems like a TODO: rich/ripe ??? for ???, right? You should be able to easily find the information relevant to the current page and be able to easily trace your jumps through the websites.

Now think what experience do you have instead. What was the last time you used it in any nontrivial way, for something other than reopening an accidentally closed tab?

Besides:

¶it's not just about web browsers

If you're using a native phone app (say, for Reddit or Twitter), your history either isn't logged anywhere or stored locally on your phone.
this is why I emphasize browsing history, not browser history
On Android, it's likely to be in an Sqlite database in /data/data/app.name. This directory is not accessible to normal users unless your phone is rooted. Just think about how ridiculous it is. It's your own data, yet your OS babysits you and prevents you from accessing it.
rooting Android phones ranges from "somewhat painful" to "very painful"

As a specific example, Reddit Slide app keeps the view history in /data/data/me.ccrama.redditslide/SEEN. Even if you root your phone and can access the database, because TODO: model is flawed something something the app isn't persisting the data in the database, it's merely keeping a few weeks cache. So if you want your complete history, you have to backup this database regularly, keep the snapshots and somehow reconstruct it.

You may dismiss this as data TODO: nitpicking? not sure what's a good word, but when all of your phone apps are doing this, you're missing out on quite a lot of information.

¶it's scattered and siloed

Continuing the previous point:

  • for the most part, you can't access history in your phone apps

  • lots of data which is ought to be counted as web history is scattered across silos

    It sits behind the APIs (best case), GDPR and manual exports (worst case) and hard to get hands on, even if you're an experienced software engineer.
    #sadinfra

    It idles on your filesystem in markdown and org-mode files.

Even regular browser history is not easy to get::

  • Firefox used to silently 'expire' your browser history

  • Chrome deletes history older than 90 days

    The only way to access older history I'm aware of is Google Activity and Takeout.

  • Google Takeout quietly recycles your history

  • Migration is limited to the most popular browsers

    The retention limits the migration as well. E.g. if you migrate from Chrome to Firefox, the history older than 90 days is locked and siloed in your Google Account.

  • You're gonna have a hard time if you're not using Google/Mozilla sync

    Your history will be scattered across devices and lost on OS reinstalls.

    There are ways of self-hosting Firefox sync, and (allegedly) even Chrome sync, but, as you can imagine, it's quite tedious.

¶it's got different weight

Not all links in your history are equal (on equal/the same grounds??).

  • some are clicked on by accident
  • some you've just skimmed
  • some are on your reading list

¶4 URLs are broken

This topic deserves a separate post TODO: someone surely must have ranted about it?, but I'll keep it a section size for now.

URLs seem great because they mostly address content, and semi-curated. In reality though:

  • links rot

    Many URLs are literally broken. We're lucky to have archive.org, so you can at least access dead pages.

    But there is no way to migrate your browser history, e.g. point to archive.org entry or a new domain. Similarly, if you had the page annotated, your annotations orphan, and usually, there is usually no easy way to re-point them.

  • obfuscated by shortening and redirects

    What happens to all the t.co links when Twitter as a service dies?
    see linkwrapping

  • obscured

    For example, there is no easy way to know what is behind https://www.instapaper.com/read/1265139707, without querying the Instapaper API.

    Relations between data are often obscured by URLs. For example, these two links:

    • https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=22918980 is a submission link
    • https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=22919718 is a comment to that submission

    These links are clearly related, but there is no way to tell it just from id.

    Compare this to Reddit links:

    • https://reddit.com/r/orgmode/comments/g6ejwe/is_there_an_orgmode_workbook_tutorial_that_is is a post link
    • https://reddit.com/r/orgmode/comments/g6ejwe/is_there_an_orgmode_workbook_tutorial_that_is/fo9qnen is a comment to that post

    The ids are obscure, but at least we can clearly see the post <-> comment relation just by looking at URL. Alas, the browsers are just ignoring this quite useful information.

  • unstandardized

    For example, queries

    • typically they don't represent anything persistent and are used for querying (duh)
    • sometimes they are used to address information: http://wiki.c2.com/?LispLanguage or https://www.scottaaronson.com/blog/?p=2694
    • and in many cases, they are utter garbage used for tracking

    The worst part is that these usecases interleave. For example, take a look at youtube.com/watch?v=wHrCkyoe72U&feature=share&time_continue=6:

    • v=wHrCkyoe72U is the most important part about this link
    • feature=share is just garbage
    • time_continue=6 could be treated as useful information

    A similar story applies to fragments:

    • on Google groups, they are meaningful and address specific discussions and messages: https://groups.google.com/a/list.hypothes.is/forum/#!topic/dev/kcmS7H8ssis
    • on most websites, they refer to the content within a page: https://github.com/lipoja/URLExtract/issues/13#issuecomment-467635302
  • unnormalized
    • just think of all the www., amp., mobile., m. garbage
    • youtu.be/1TKSfAkWWN0, youtube.com/embed/1TKSfAkWWN0 and https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1TKSfAkWWN0&list=WL&index=11 refer to the exact same content, yet your browser has no idea.
  • <link rel="canonical">
    • often it's not not present
    • when it does, often not used properly
    • TODO: can't know it without fetching the page first? -- elaborate on this?

I want URLs to address information and represent relations. The current URL experience is far from ideal for this.

¶Prototypes and mockups

  • "Margin Notes: Building a Contextually Aware Associative Memory"

    A paper from 2000 (!):

    Margin Notes is a remembrance agent that automatically rewrites web pages as they are loaded, adding hyperlinks to personal files.

    This page has more information and this seems like more of a recommendation system, and I haven't found any source code to check it out.

  • Historia by Mek

    Goal: use a chrome extension to supplement your browsing experience by introducing a superior browsing history data structure:

    • preserve provenance. maintain a detailed account of browsing history
    • track interactions with dynamic content

    Sadly it's only a concept.

  • Memacs by Karl Voit

    Memacs is a memex, which unifies your data as org-mode, allowing for querying in from within Emacs.

    Unfortunately, that means you have to query information actively in your emacs, as opposed to a passive assistant that does it for you.

    In addition, keeping everything in org-mode will be inefficient for long browsing histories with hundreds of thousands of entries.

  • Memex by Andrew Louis

    This is a working (but not open-source, at least yet) prototype of a Memex, unifying personal data and exposing an API for it.

    I only ran at it a few months ago! Because it's not public, I'm not sure to which extent it can address the browser history problem specifically. But jumping a bit ahead, I expect it to integrate well with Promnesia, so we could benefit from each other's work.

¶'Unlimited history' extensions

There is a family of Chrome extensions similar to History Trends Unlimited, which try to make up for Chrome 90-day retention by keeping the history in IndexDB. The only problem they solve is the said retention, and it ends up as a yet another silo.

But at least, thousands of users means there is a demand for such functionality.

¶Google Activity/Takeout

I mentioned it already, but considering that many people use it, it worth a separate mention. Its problems are:

  • it's a Google-specific silo, and the functionality provided by Google is only useful for deliberate searches
  • it doesn't have an API, and can't be integrated in the browser

¶Worldbrain Memex

Worldbrain Memex is a very cool browser extension. It's open source, keeps your history, supports tagging, highlights, annotation, and even full-text search. By all means, try it!

The only problem is that Memex is that it's also kind of a silo. I really hate using the word "silo" here, because in my mind it's an insult. If anything, Memex is probably the nicest silo I know of. It keeps data locally, it's got data imports from other services.

But nevertheless, if you commit to using it, you have to move the data into Memex, and conform it to its model.

If for some reason you can't or don't want to use Memex for annotating and recording all of your history (e.g. on some of your devices) – you TODO: what did I want to say here?

In summary, a nice tool is great, but what I want is a different model of interacting with my existing data, which doesn't require moving the data in-between the silos. (see "it's not just about the browser")

And jumping ahead again, Promnesia can be used with Memex data. In the future, I'll check out if we can benefit from each other's work.

¶6 Hello, Promnesia!

Ok enough rants! Promnesia is the tool I've developed to address some of the issues with the browsing history I've mentioned.

I've already mentioned what I wanted, but I'll recap:

  • make the history useful: unify scattered and siloed history under a single interface

    There should be absolutely no difference between the link you opened yesterday in your Instapaper phone app, and a link you visited in Firefox ten years ago.

  • make the URLs useful: benefit from the hierarchy and relations between them
  • assist me at exploring new information

  • make it possible to easily trace through the history

    Let me answer, when, how, and why have I visited/bookmarked the page?

  • integrate the metadata: annotations, highlights, notes, chat messages, tweets, etc. Display it right within the page when applicable (e.g. highlights)

    Make webpages context-aware and embed the TODO: other digital?? walled gardens? TODO: release from silos?

  • make it flexible

    As long as it's something you can extract URLs from, you should be able to feed it in Promnesia.

Here, I'm cheating a little bit, because some of these goals weren't clear from the beginning – I had understood them during the development.

There are also some meta-goals and principles I'm adhering:

  • make it open source

    That goes without saying, everything I'm doing for this project is shared and open source. Francly, I don't believe this can be sustainable any other way.

  • local first

    Your past is sensitive data and you should have control where do you keep it and how you're accessing it. In the simplest setup, promnesia talks to your browser through a local port.

    TODO: say smth about performance etc

    TODO: it's even possible to use it offline (if your browser supported offline)

  • make it modular

    Make sure the components can be reused.

    For the last several months, I've been mostly removing/moving away the code from Promnesia, to make sure that the data processing and normalization code used in Promnesia can be used for other purposes by other people.

    I'm going to take this even further and perhaps try to see if parts of Promnesia can be useful for:

    • Hypothes.is project
    • Worldbrain Memex project

  • don't build another silo, use existing information

    This kind of complements the previous point.

    There are many decent services and tools already. They all have their own pros and cons. Different people prefer different tool, sometimes because they're locked in and don't have much choice, but sometimes because they genuinely have subtle preferences and opinions.

    The cost of switching is too high, both in terms of the time and the cognitive effort. There's always a risk that you'd encounter this one small thing that would make the tool a no-go.

    We should invest in finding better ways of combining existing tools, not rewriting all over again.

¶7 How can you use Promnesia?

This is how I'm using it. The README also has screenshots and short screencasts which explain different features.

  • Finding new information

    Example: I run into an article on Twitter. I enjoyed it, and the website looks kind of familiar.

    When I jump on https://nautil.us, the Promnesia sidebar shows me that I've visited and even annotated other articles on this site before!

    So it seems worthy of an RSS subscription, which I do!

  • Exploring people and communities

    • How often have I engaged with a certain blog in the past?

      Which posts have I already seen or read? Did I like them? Should I dive deeper?

    • Same questions, but for twitter accounts, subreddits, youtube channels or anything similar

  • Processing large amounts of information

    For example:

    • Pinboard popular
    • link compilations
    • Hackernews 'classics'
  • Display annotations and highlights from any service (e.g. Instapaper/Pocket), within the webpage
  • Personal digital archaeology and external memory
    • Why did I bookmark this youtube video? Who sent it to me?
    • How did I get on this forgotten tab?

    • I'm sometimes annoyed by @<account> tweets. Should I unfollow them?
