So, what's wrong with the existing browser history?

¶1 What is wrong with browser history?

Browsing history is a very rich potentially source of useful information. Just think about it: it contains exact timestamps and exact links which basically address information. To some extensn URLs can express relationships (TODO to some extent) like persons/groups/TODO?? we have for free.

Seems like a rich/ripe TODO for TODO.

You should be able to know TODO, associate TODO, be able to trace your jumps through the websites.

Now think what experience do you have instead. What was the last time you used it in any nontrivial way, for something other than reopening an accidentally closed tab?

In addition:

¶it's not just about web browser

For example, if you're using a native phone app for Facebook or Reddit, you history isn't logged anywhere or stored in the database (TODO /data/data/)

Database is not accessible to normal users, unless your phone is rooted.
rooting modern Android phones ranges from "somewhat painful" to "very painful"

Just think about how ridiculous it is. It's your own data, yet your phone OS babysits you and prevents your from accessing it. Yes, I know about sandboxing and TODO.

¶it's scattered and siloed

  • for the most part, you can't access history in your phone apps (non-browser)
  • TODO link to chrome/firefox history recycling policy

  • if you're not logged into Google account/Mozilla sync

    There are ways of running Firefox, and allegedly even Chrome sync

  • Google Takout won't save you TODO link
  • if you're using different browsers on different devices, good luck
  • TODO browser interop Although these days
  • TODO annotation data

¶not all of it is equal

Not all pages in your history are the same.

  • large proportion are just full of tracking TODO garbage
  • some are clicked on by accident
  • some you've just skimmed
  • some you've been reading for hours and they are full of your highlights and annotations
  • some you are keeping in your personal wiki
  • some of them you've shared with other people on social media

The current browser history experience makes absolutely no difference between these scenarios.

I'd say the biggest problem with something like this is that it's a silo. You're suddenly 100% reliant on them providing the right tools and functions to access your data in the way you want/need. And if your needs/preferences change, then you're entirely reliant on whether the silo has foreseen the new use case.

I already have an existing knowledge base (that not only consists of webpages, but also org files, videos, pdf's, etc.) that's accessible and synchronized across multiple devices - and while having a complete searchable history of all my browsing would be fantastic, there's no way to integrate it into my system (or any other system) with my own tools.
